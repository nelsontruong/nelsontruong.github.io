Context and Problem
    Fiat Chrysler Automotives
    • Consists of 5 international brands operating independently resulting in a design inconsistenices, unalignment, and slow turnaround time to update basic vehicle pages.
My Role
    Architecting components for brand teams designed for 4 breakpoints - Desktop, Tablet, Phablet, Mobile
    • This involved gathering of user requirements, determining brand and user value, performing a competitive experience analysis and determining if the component aligns with the philosophy of the design system (such re-using previous subcomponents, re-using defined colors, fonts, and if it should be made as a variation of an exisitng component or a new component).
      The design of a component enables speed and velocity when authoring content. An authored image only needs to be defined once and it will automatically adjust accordingly maintaining its aspect ratio on smaller breakpoints. The same goes for font sizes and subcomponents like an arrow.
    Whiteboarding with Visual Designers and Developers
    • Whiteboarding sessions were held for components in conjunction with visual designers and developers. Whiteboarding replaced the need to make wires for many components. Any issues with UX, Development, or Visual Design are discussed.

      Because of a componentized framework within a design system, we must always design for the worst case scenario from the content authors perspective (shoving as much content into the component) and consider the systems requirements hosted from FCA server-side.
    Working with Visual Designers to Refine the End Product
    • On the FCA Product Team, UX and Visual Designers work in tandem to go straight from the whiteboarding phase directly to visual design. Wires are made for complex components, and interactions not defined in the design system framework.
    Documenting the final design requirements and syncing with the Business Analyst
    • We start off by documenting the general structure of a component where documentation is done by linking subcomponents with a complex component design. Attributes in components are inherited or specified by the CMS author.

      The example below is a component map made for a Feature Panel. A feature panel inherits traits from subcomponents such as a display media and is composed of other subcomponents such as the description block. The description block has its own set of unique attributes. The component map provides a high level overview of the component structure.

      To simplify things for developers and QA teams, visual annotations are made:

      Front-end requirements would be synced and aligned with back-end requirements.

      Accessibility requirements are then defined such as specifying the Aria-Live region of where a screenreader would read out to a user where there would occur an action or state change.
Challenges
    Brands teams not understanding a design system
    • One change permeates throughout the rest of the experience for other brands. For example, if a team wanted to change the core functionality of a component, they didn't realize it would effect other brands using the same component. I mitigated this by continuously communicating with the brand teams and put myself forward as the go-to person for design system questions.
    Responsive approach for all components
    • There are some components where content requirements or asset sizing cause problems on smaller breakpoints. An example is the hero. On Desktop, the image is long and wide but on mobile, the same image would not appear well. To compensate for this, the team modified the hero be adaptive on smaller breakpoints to be tall and narrow.
      Hero Image that is used on all breakpoints in our Responsive Hero:

      Hero Image designed to be adaptive:
    Offshore Development
    • Working with developers in India present unique challenges to time zones and cultural differences. When we received the platform back, there were big gaps in the expectation of quality. Part of the reason is that the design team operated under the assumption that the development team would implement assumed features (such as if a tab is in its selected state, selecting another tab would move the selected state to it). I've since learned to be as prescriptive as possible and operate under no assumptions for developers. This has yielded the highest quality of work.
    Usability Testing to validate design
    • Unfortunately, this was a problem I could not overcome as the client was unwilling to invest money into user testing. As a result, many of our designs were only validated internally and largely by the product owners client side.
Results
    A consistent experience across 7 brands done within a year. My manager put it best. Since we were going so fast, it was like flying an airplane but building it at the same time. Click through rates jumped drastically but most importantly - speed, this saved huge amounts of effort in terms of development, content creation, and design. Strategically, it allowed FCA to focus more efforts on vehicle tools to offer user value, campaigns, launches, etc.

Context and Problem
    FCA International - Japan, Korea, India, Caribbean, Argentina
  • Inefficiencies and inability to scale their digital platform similar to the U.S. market site. The goal was to bring the platform on board and fully operating international sites within a 5 month period (2 months design, 3 months development and content authoring)
My Role
    Lead UX and architect international sites and design (or modify) components for each FCA International Market
  • Design pages for the international site using existing components if possible. I would design new components if necessary. The wire shown below is made in Sketch and an example that I collaborated with another UX designer Jenna as we jammed through all the rest of the international market pages.
  • How did we structure the pages and why did we do them a certain way? We examined existing web pages and mapped those web pages on how they would be created using our current component framework. We then modified the page with different components (or enhancements to compoenents) and created templates in Excel to keep track of all variations. In total, we had over 50 templates for the International markets, most being one-off pages.

  • We followed this method of using components that would work with their current design as a way of providing our international clients with an experience they are familiar and already aligned with. If there are design or usability improvements, we reflect that in the templates and discussions. Using Excel provided us an organized method to keep track of all the site URLs we had to re-design. The below image shows an example of half a worksheet for one market.
    Take charge of multiple roles and also fill the role of a Business Analyst
  • At the start of the project, there was a gap where the BA position was not filled by anyone. As a result, I would also architect the back-end requirements and also gather the functional / user requirements in addition to my role as the lead UX designer on this project.
    Establish a working design relationship with clients
  • Since the international clients were overseas in various countries, it was difficult to do scrum or agile sessions. The approach for this project was more in line with a waterfall method and we had regular reviews with the client where I would showcase component and page designs via conferencing. The pain point was that before presenting, there were many cases where I wasn't able to see the final page provided by the content author as the team was truly global. Many of the international clients were located in Asia-Pacific, Content Authors and Developers in India, and I'm situated in Toronto.
Challenges
    Lack of Cultural Context and Understanding
    • One of my goals in the beginning was to do a Netnography and User Testing to understand how users in different areas of the world would use automobile sites differently. Due to budget constraints and user testing being a low priority for FCA, this was not moved forward.
    Around the world communication
    • Clients were located in Asia, Development Team located in India, Design Team located in Toronto. It was a challenging coordinating efforts and resulted in lots of wasted time waiting for client approval.
    Organization
    • The design team was tasked not only with design, but to organize all the URLs and to keep track of progress ourselves. With 300+ sites, this became very difficult at first to match the sites to corresponding templates. Even more difficult when the client came back and wanted to change templates affecting multiple sites. As a result, we simplified by working in batches and only changing templates when absolutely necessary.
Results
    I also had fantastic team members for visual design and UX to help complete this project together - Yuko, Jenna, Karen, Joyce.

Other Projects

RAM Commercial Custom Graphics Tool

Context and What I Did
  • RAM Commercial wanted a software to be used at dealerships to show small to medium sized business owners what they're logo would look like on a RAM. It's like a Microsoft Paint Tool that you can use on ramtrucks.com. The purpose of this tool wasn't to let users design in detail of what they're truck would appear like, but rather as an exploration tool to get them excited and imagine their business logo and design on a RAMTruck.
My Role
    UX lead for this project.
  • This involved gathering the requirements, leading the interaction design and execution for this project.
Challenges
    No user testing or user research
    • The client was not willing to pay for it, so this tool was designed on open-ended requirements and key assumptions provided by the RAM client.
    Responsiveness on 4 breakpoints like before
    • Not all functionalities worked well on Mobile, especially when configuring with tools that were detailed oriented (such as adding custom text). Additionally, adding in many visual layers (text, images, etc.) do not meet the system requirements as it would be very slow on mobile phones. As a solution, a adaptive approach for some functionalities (such as adding custom text) were removed from phablet and mobile. This provided a better streamlined experience on mobile but still allowed the user to use and play with features that were most important to them. Additionally with the small canvas area, adding in custom text and changing the fonts would not be very noticeable and may take away from the overall purpose of this tool.
Results
  • The client and dealerships were excited about the simplicity, but functionality of this tool. The ability to put any image on a truck, change its shape, add custom decals and colors on desktop, tablet and mobile were very positive feedback given to us.
Cadillac Fairview Santa Signup

What was Designed:
Context and What I Did
    Lead UX to increase Click Through Rates and streamline user registration for Santa Photo Signup at Cadillac Fairview Malls

  • My recommendation were 2 options:

    • Option 1) Move the Calendar to the top and shorten the "Visit with Santa Module" to only include key dates and remove extra line spacing. This would push the calendar to be above the fold. Users are able to see key dates as they are choosing the date on the calendar.

    • Option 2) Change the Visit with Santa module to be only a header module (cannot change the Blue "Events" banner for the purposes of site hierarchy). Move the calendar up so it is in the view of the user's viewport on initial page load and it implies for for the user.
  • In the end, the client was very persistent about keeping the "Visit with Santa" module at the top with key dates clearly outlined. This was similar to last year's approach that they were comfortable with.

  • The design of the calendar featured blue labels and colouring for any interactive elements, such as changing the month or selecting tabs. Dates were in an inactive state. On hover of the date items changed to a light blue color with the active state using a darker blue. A special accessibility tool (Chrome addon) was used to examine contrast and we were AA compliant. On the right side of the calendar are time slots with corresponding icons to choose throughout the day. If there were no times in the "morning", "afternoon", or "evening" tab, those tabs would be disabled. If all morning slots were filled or none available, then it would default the user to the next available tab and the morning tab would be disabled.


  • Why did we divide it up the time intervals? There were many days where there would be 15 mins pers signup slot for 10+ hours. This meant the user would have to scroll a lot in order to reach times at night. To mitigate this, tabs corresponding to morning, afternoon and evening with text labels to show the time were implemented. We also used terms such as "Open" to 12:00 PM and 6:00 PM to "Close". Open and Close were used because malls would open and close at different times. It would be too much of a development effort to retrieve the opening and closing hours of each mall dynamically. Once the user clicks on "reserve", it redirected the user to an event registration "e-commerce checkout" page. At this point there was no more design input as the checkout page was handled by a 3rd party vendor.

    • What was Implemented (Challenges):

    • The top section ended up containing a lot of content pushing the calendar far down the page. Although design did give content direction, the client ultimately decided the copy. As a designer, this is partially my responsibility in convincing the client. Additionally, the design requirement of disabling tabs in the day that did not have any openings were never disabled. In the visual above, December 10th only has openings in the evening, but the morning, and afternoon tabs were never disabled and the evening tab is not defaulted to the selected state.
